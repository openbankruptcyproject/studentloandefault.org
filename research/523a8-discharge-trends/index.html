§ 523(a)(8) Adversary Filings — National Tracker

Borrowers attempting to discharge student loans in bankruptcy must file a separate civil lawsuit — an adversary proceeding — under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8). This is the running national count, by district and by year, with a focused look at the period before and after the November 2022 DOJ guidance.

First full year post-guidance

The Justice Department issued new guidance on November 17, 2022, instructing U.S. Trustees to use a streamlined, two-document attestation process and a more permissive standard when evaluating borrower claims of "undue hardship." The empirical question — whether borrowers responded to that change — has not been answered nationally.

This page tracks the inputs to that question. Outcome classification — whether each adversary proceeding ended in a full discharge, partial discharge, settlement, or denial — is the second phase of this project, scheduled to ship in mid-May 2026.

What the curve shows

  • 2014–2021: roughly flat at 260–300 filings per year — the long-running baseline, even as the discharge bar (the Brunner-test "undue hardship" standard) had been widely treated as effectively unavailable.
  • 2022: a dip to 227, plausibly reflecting borrowers and counsel waiting to see what the anticipated DOJ guidance would say.
  • 2023: 397 filings, a single-year record in the indexed window — the first full calendar year operating under the new guidance.
  • 2024: 325, still elevated above the historical baseline.
  • 2025–2026: indexing lag — RECAP receives filings from PACER on a rolling basis, and recent filings are systematically undercounted at the time of any given snapshot.

Methodology

Data source

Counts are pulled from the Free Law Project's CourtListener RECAP archive — a public, searchable mirror of federal court documents originally obtained from PACER. Query, endpoint, and counts are reproducible by anyone:

  • Endpoint: https://www.courtlistener.com/api/rest/v4/search/
  • Query: q="523(a)(8)"
  • Type: type=r (RECAP filings index)
  • Date filters: filed_before / filed_after for the November 17, 2022 cutoff
  • District filter: court=<court_id> using CourtListener's bankruptcy-court IDs (those ending in b)

What's being counted

A "filing" here means a docket where the text "523(a)(8)" appears in any indexed document — which captures the adversary proceedings themselves, plus complaints, answers, motions, and orders that cite the statute. The number is best read as the volume of federal court activity centered on student-loan dischargeability rather than a strict count of lawsuit-by-lawsuit filings. Outcome classification (Phase 2) will resolve this to one row per adversary proceeding.

The DOJ guidance cutoff

On November 17, 2022, the U.S. Department of Justice's Civil Division and the Executive Office for U.S. Trustees jointly issued Guidance for Department Attorneys Regarding Student Loan Bankruptcy Litigation. The guidance directs DOJ attorneys to use a debtor attestation form and a clearer "undue hardship" framework when evaluating consent to discharge. We use that date — 2022-11-17 — as the pre/post boundary.

Caveats

RECAP indexing lag. RECAP receives federal docket data from PACER on a rolling basis driven by user uploads, court-watcher tooling, and ongoing scraping by the Free Law Project. Recently filed adversary proceedings — especially in the past 6 to 12 months — are systematically undercounted at any given snapshot. The 2025 and 2026 figures will rise as RECAP catches up; treat them as floors, not ceilings.

Filings, not outcomes. A high filing count in a district does not mean a high discharge rate in that district. Outcome classification — full discharge, partial discharge, settlement, denial, or pending — is the next phase of this project.

Pro se vs. represented. The most consequential disparity in student-loan-bankruptcy outcomes is whether the borrower has counsel. Phase 2 will code each adversary by representation status using the CourtListener party/attorney metadata.

What Phase 2 will add (mid-May 2026)

  1. Outcome classification. For each of the ~5,070 adversary proceedings, the disposition: discharged, partial discharge, settled, denied, dismissed, or pending. Mix of automated parsing of final orders and human review of edge cases.
  2. Pre-vs-post discharge rates. Same denominator, same query, before and after November 17, 2022 — the comparison nobody has published nationally.
  3. Pro-se vs. represented success rates. Coded from CourtListener party/attorney records.
  4. Interactive district map. Same tooling already used by the Open Bankruptcy Project's other empirical maps.
  5. CSV export. Per-AP, with case caption, court, filing date, disposition, representation status.
